# design-it-twice: where the crossing's totality guarantee lives [machine]

A load-bearing interface for "where `worker.run`'s totality guarantee lives" was designed twice —
candidates worker-boundary and scheduler-only-entry, each isolated as its own brief and judged on
depth, locality, and seam placement. It is load-bearing because `worker.run`'s own docstring already
advertises "the whole crossing, one terminal," and the seam decides whether that promise is true by
construction or only under a wrapper — the same promise the system's central trust claim rests on
(intent §60/§62; `engine/schedule.py:24-27`). The fork is real: today the guarantee is split — the
worker recovers-and-re-raises (`worker.py:304-306`) and only `Scheduler._fail` raises the card
(`schedule.py:136-142`), so a bare `worker.run` that raises loses the failure with no card.

design-decision: where the crossing's totality guarantee lives → worker-boundary (`worker.run` raises its own parented decision card) — `worker.run` advertises "the whole crossing, one terminal" in its own docstring, so the guarantee belongs at that boundary: its `except` raises the could-not-complete decision card parented to the node (carrying the operator's fork), recovers the node, then re-raises, so the parented card blocks re-dispatch for every caller — fenced, hand-driven, or scheduled — and `Scheduler._fail`'s now-redundant second copy is removed, leaving the totality logic in exactly one place; B is rejected because it makes `worker.run` a trap whose docstring is true only by an unenforced convention, keeps the guarantee split across two modules, and forces the documented hand-driven dispatch path to spin up the scheduler's lease/threading just to crank one node.

## Grounds

- **worker-boundary — `worker.run` raises its own decision card before re-raising (chosen)**: the guarantee moves into `worker.run`'s `except`: raise the parented could-not-complete card (the same operator fork `_fail` composed — abandon / re-cut / change the ask), recover the node, re-raise. `Scheduler._fail` is deleted and `_work` collapses to `worker.run(...)` swallowing the re-raise (the card is already on the queue; the loop survives). Deepest on seam placement: it makes `worker.run`'s advertised "whole crossing, one terminal" true by construction for *every* caller, never only the scheduler — the docstring stops over-promising. Deepest on locality: the two halves of "a failed crossing leaves a blocked, carded node" (`tree.recover` and the card-raise) sit in one `except`, where today they are split across `worker.py` and `schedule.py`. Errors are defined out of existence for the caller: no bare caller must remember to wrap `worker.run` in a scheduler to be total. The totality logic lives once — the redundant `_fail` copy deletes cleanly with nothing left behind. The documented hand-driven dispatch path (`worker.run(tree.find(id))`) keeps working unchanged, now total.
- **scheduler-only-entry — keep the split, forbid any bare `worker.run` caller (rejected)**: leave `worker.run` recovering-and-re-raising and `_fail` raising the card; make the scheduler the only legal entry and route the dispatch command through `Scheduler.step()`. Rejected on depth and seam placement: it leaves `worker.run`'s docstring a lie guarded only by a comment — a trap interface that exposes a method unsafe to call directly, the opposite of pulling complexity downward. Rejected on locality: the totality logic stays split across two modules, so a reader must hold both `worker.run` and `Scheduler._fail` to know what a failed crossing does. Rejected on cost: it forces the documented single-node hand-crank to drag in the scheduler's lease and threading machinery for a job that wants none, and it cannot even satisfy this node's own red→green scenario, which presumes a *bare* `worker.run` leaves a card — B would have to assert the bare path is forbidden, a weaker and different contract.
